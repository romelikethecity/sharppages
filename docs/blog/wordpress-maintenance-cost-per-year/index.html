What WordPress Maintenance Actually Costs Per Year (2026)

By Rome Thorndike

WordPress Is Free. Running It Is Not.

WordPress.org is open-source software. You can download it for nothing. But running a WordPress site requires hosting, a domain, SSL, plugins, themes, security monitoring, backups, and ongoing updates. Most business owners underestimate the total annual cost because the expenses are spread across monthly subscriptions, annual renewals, and occasional developer invoices that arrive without warning.

When someone says "my website costs $20 a month," they are counting hosting and nothing else. The real number is usually 5x to 10x higher once you add everything required to keep a WordPress site secure, functional, and reasonably fast.

Here is the full breakdown for 2026, from a minimal DIY setup to a properly maintained business site.

The Annual Cost Breakdown

Category Low End Mid Range High End
Hosting $120/yr $360/yr $1,200/yr
Domain + SSL $15/yr $15/yr $50/yr
Premium Theme $0 $60/yr $200/yr
Plugins (premium) $0 $200/yr $800/yr
Security (Sucuri/Wordfence) $0 $200/yr $500/yr
Backups (UpdraftPlus/VaultPress) $0 $100/yr $240/yr
Developer for Updates $0 (DIY) $600/yr $3,000/yr
Emergency Fixes $0 $500/yr $2,000/yr
TOTAL $135/yr $2,035/yr $7,990/yr

The low end represents a DIY site running free plugins on shared hosting. This is the personal blog or side project setup. The mid range is a typical small business site with premium plugins, managed hosting, and occasional developer help. The high end is a site that depends on managed WordPress hosting (WP Engine, Kinsta), multiple premium plugins, and a developer on retainer for regular maintenance.

Most business websites fall in the mid range. If you have a developer you call when things break, a security plugin, and 3 or more premium plugins, you are spending $2,000 to $4,000 per year whether you realize it or not.

The Costs Nobody Mentions

The table above covers direct, predictable expenses. But WordPress has a second category of costs that do not show up on invoices until something goes wrong.

1. Plugin conflicts after updates. WordPress core releases major updates 3 to 4 times per year. Each update can break plugin compatibility. A form plugin that worked perfectly on WordPress 6.5 might throw errors on 6.6. A page builder that rendered correctly might shift layouts after a PHP update. Debugging plugin conflicts takes developer time, typically $100 to $300 per incident. Most business sites experience 2 to 4 plugin conflicts per year.

2. Security vulnerabilities. WordPress powers over 40% of the web, which makes it the single biggest target for automated attacks. Outdated plugins are the primary attack vector. A 2023 Patchstack report found that 97% of WordPress security vulnerabilities came from plugins, not WordPress core. One breach can cost thousands in cleanup, lost customer trust, and potential data liability. If you are collecting form submissions with customer data, a breach has legal implications under GDPR and state privacy laws.

3. Performance degradation over time. Every plugin adds JavaScript, CSS, and database queries to every page load. WordPress sites start fast and slow down gradually as plugins accumulate and the database grows. By year 2 or 3, most WordPress sites score 50 to 70 on mobile PageSpeed. The caching plugin fights the bloat, but it is treating symptoms, not the cause. Rebuilding performance means auditing every plugin and often removing features you paid for. Read more about why WordPress sites slow down.

4. Opportunity cost. Hours spent managing WordPress (running updates, testing after updates, restoring backups, troubleshooting plugin issues, monitoring security alerts) are hours not spent on your business. For a business owner managing their own site, this is typically 2 to 5 hours per month. For a team delegating to a developer, it is budget allocated to maintenance instead of growth.

5. Platform lock-in through complexity. The longer you run WordPress, the more entangled your content becomes with specific plugins. Your forms depend on Gravity Forms. Your layouts depend on Elementor. Your SEO settings live in Yoast. Migrating away from WordPress means untangling all of these dependencies. The switching cost grows every year, which is why many businesses stay on WordPress longer than they should.

The Static Alternative

A static HTML website eliminates nearly every recurring cost in the table above.

Hosting: $0 per month. Static sites can be hosted for free on GitHub Pages or Cloudflare Pages. No server to manage. No database to maintain. No PHP to patch. The CDN serves your files globally with near-instant load times.

No plugins to update. Static sites do not have plugins. Functionality like contact forms, analytics, and newsletter signups are handled through lightweight external services (Formspree, Google Analytics, Mailchimp) that maintain themselves. There is no plugin ecosystem to monitor, update, or debug.

No database to secure. With no MySQL database, there is no SQL injection vector. With no admin login page, there is no brute force target. With no PHP execution, there is no remote code execution risk. The attack surface of a static site is essentially zero.

Annual maintenance cost: effectively $15 per year. That covers domain renewal. SSL is free through the hosting provider. There is nothing else to pay for. No security monitoring. No backup service. No developer retainer for updates.

One-time build cost: $1,500 to $5,000 depending on scope. A 5-page business site starts at $1,500. A 15 to 30 page site with custom design, schema markup, and full SEO optimization runs $3,000 to $5,000. That one-time investment pays for itself within 6 to 18 months compared to WordPress maintenance costs. Learn more about our web design service or WordPress migration service.

Over 3 years, the math is clear. A WordPress site at the mid range costs roughly $6,100 in maintenance alone ($2,035 times 3). A static site costs $45 ($15 times 3 for domain renewals) plus the one-time build. Even at $5,000 for the build, you spend $5,045 total over 3 years, and that includes getting a brand new, faster website.

When WordPress Still Makes Sense

WordPress is not the right choice for most business websites. But there are cases where it still makes sense, and being honest about that matters.

Daily content publishing by non-technical staff. If you have a team of writers publishing 3 or more articles per week and they need a visual editor with draft workflows and scheduling, a CMS provides real value. Static site generators can handle blogs, but they require more technical comfort from content authors.

Complex e-commerce. WooCommerce handles product catalogs, inventory management, payment processing, and shipping calculations in one integrated system. For stores with hundreds of SKUs, variable pricing, and complex fulfillment logic, WordPress with WooCommerce is a legitimate choice. Simple stores with under 50 products have better alternatives.

Specific plugin dependencies. Some WordPress plugins have no equivalent outside the ecosystem. LMS plugins like LearnDash for course delivery, membership plugins with complex access tiers, and booking systems with calendar integrations are examples. If your business depends on a specific WordPress plugin, that dependency is a real factor.

Sites with 1,000+ frequently updated pages. Large content sites with hundreds of pages updated weekly benefit from a database-driven CMS. Managing that volume through static files is possible but requires a build system and more technical infrastructure.

For most business websites (5 to 30 pages, updated monthly or less), WordPress is overkill. You are paying for a content management system when what you need is a website.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is WordPress really more expensive than a static site over time?

For most business websites, yes. A static site costs $1,500 to $5,000 once, then $15 per year for the domain. WordPress costs $2,000 to $8,000 per year in hosting, plugins, security, and maintenance. The static site pays for itself within the first year or two.

Can I migrate my WordPress site without losing SEO?

Yes, if done correctly. Proper redirects, matching URL structures, and transferring all meta tags preserves your search rankings. We handle this as part of our redesign service. See our migration guide for details.

What if I need to make changes to my static site later?

You own all the HTML, CSS, and JavaScript files. Any developer, or you with basic HTML knowledge, can edit them. There is no platform lock-in. If you need ongoing changes, we offer maintenance packages or you can manage the files yourself.
